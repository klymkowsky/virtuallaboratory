Cell Shape & the Cytoskeleton

In contrast to prokaryotic cells, whose shapes are determined largely by the shape of their cell walls, animal cells can assume a number of shapes.

More to the point they can actively change shape.

They can grow to large sizes and move materials large distances. For example neurotransmitter-containing vesicles found at synapses are transported from the neuronal soma down the axon, sometimes as much as a meter or more.

 
 
 

The shape and mobility of animal cells is dependent upon a set of interconnected protein polymer networks, known collectively as the cytoskeleton.

There are five major cytoskeletal polymer types.

  • actin filaments
  • myosin filaments
  • microtubules
  • intermediate filaments and
  • lamin networks
 

Cytoskeletal polymers can be visualized using fluorescent reagents that bind to specific cytoskeletal subunit proteins.

When illuminated with light of the appropriate wavelength, the labeled polymers will fluorescence.

Using the appropriate filters, and a microscope, we can is see this fluorescence.

Reagents labeled with different fluorophores are used to visualize the organization of multiple cytoskeletal systems simultaneously.

Actin is a very highly conserved polypeptide - there are only a few amino acids changes between the actins of plants, yeasts and mammals.

The actin molecule is roughly globular.

Within the cell it exists in two distinct forms: G- or globular actin and F- or filamentous actin.

In an actin filament, all of the actin polypeptides point in the same direction.

The actin filament has an unambiguous directionality; its two ends are different.

The polymer is said to be polar. In this context, polar means that a molecule, looking at an actin filament's surface, can tell which way the filament is "pointing".

 

 

Typically within a cell, the total amounts of G-actin and F-actin do not change - they are in a steady state. About half of the total actin is in each state.

This does not mean, however, that the system is static. In fact, it is quite dynamic.

This dynamic behavior is due to four properties of actin.

  • Actin exists bound to either ATP or ADP. Actin without bound nucleotide is not found in cells, presumably because it is not stable.
  • G-Actin is almost exclusively in the Actin-ATP state. G-Actin-ADP is rapidly converted into G-Actin-ATP by exchanging its ADP for an ATP.
  • Actin is an ATPase
  • Actin's ATPase activity increases when it is assembled into a filament

This together with the polar organization of the actin filament these produce two distinct behaviors.

 

The two end of the actin filament differ. When high concentrations of actin monomer are present, both ends of the filament will grow.

One end, however, grows faster than the other.

 

The faster assembling end of the filament is known as the "+" end, the slower assembling end the "-" end.

 
 

At steady state, the rate of assembly at the + end is equal to the rate of disassembly at the - end.

This leads to a behavior known as treadmilling. The polymer can move, work is performed

Tread milling requires ATP hydrolysis. While the polymer moves, the subunits within the polymer do not.

 

Actin assembly and filament organisms are regulated by a number of accessory proteins.

Among the most critical are proteins regulate the formation and growth of filaments.

One of these proteins is called profilin. The actin/profilin complex is known as profilactin.

Profilin blocks the de novo assembly of actin filaments. It is use to enable cells to maintain actin in a monomeric state.

Disruption of the profilin/actin complex can then be used to trigger the explosive polymerization of actin monomers into filamentous actin. .

 

Actin-associate proteins can

  • facilitate the initiation of a new filaments, a process known as nucleation.
  • They can cap and stabilize the ends of an actin filament
  • Some will cross-link filaments and
  • some can cut or severe filaments.
  • They can generate contractile movement or
  • explosive polymerization or
  • mediate interactions with membrane proteins and other cytoskeletal systems.

Together with actin, these proteins generate a dynamic filament network that can move the cell forward and produce complex cellular movements.

see a neurophil chasing a bacterium

Dynamic actin filament networks are responsible for the extensive or forward moving- component of cell motility.

Actin networks can also generate contractile movement, in concert with myosins. There are over 20 different types of myosins in humans.

 

Type II myosins form their own type of filaments, known as thick filaments.

These are characteristic of muscle tissues.

They form filaments of bipolar nature.

 
 
 

Almost all of the myosins use the energy released by ATP hydrolysis to walk along actin filaments towards the actin filament's "+" end.

In muscle, a bipolar myosin filament walks along two actin filaments oriented in opposite directions.

 

As a result the muscle contracts.

Muscles only contract and do not actively extend, although they do have a elastic or passive component.

This is why muscles are always found as opposing pairs.

The active contraction of one member of the pair passively extends the other member of the pair.

In tetanus or lockjaw, muscle coordination is lost. Both members of muscle pairs contract at the same time. The body becomes rigid.
 

Actin-myosin mediated contraction is responsible for cytokinesis.

A contractile ring of actin and myosin filaments assemble at the equator of the cell.

As this cleavage furrow contracts is pinches the cell into two.

 

Other types of myosins, called non-muscle or unconventional myosins, play a number of important roles in non-muscle tissues.

For example, myosin VI is found in the inner ear. Mutations in the myosin VI gene lead to deafness.

Another actin-dependent motile mechanism is used to harpoon eggs in the horseshoe crab Limulus.

Here, the actin-binding protein scruin binds to, and bundles, actin filaments into a tight spring, wrapped around the base of the sperm nucleus.

Upon sperm/egg interaction intracellular [Ca2+] increases leading to a change in scruin's conformation.

The actin spring uncoils, passing through a channel in the nucleus and extends its full 60 µm length, impaling the egg like a harpoon.
