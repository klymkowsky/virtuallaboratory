Why virtual laboratories?

Are they as good as the "real" thing? and how to do independent study

Origins: The laboratory course appears to its origins with the anatomical and surgical demonstration.

Lab courses are good for learning the practicalities of specific techniques.

Unfortunately, and for reasons that have little to do with teaching efficacy, they have morphed into the ubiquitous required undergraduate lab course(s).

It may surprise you to learn that most working scientists I know, myself included, disliked lab courses intensely.

Real scientific research is a time-consuming, repetitive, frustrating and, for some, addictive endeavor. Experiments fail for all sorts of reasons.

By understanding both our failures and our successes we come to a working understanding the system under study.

The artificial constraints under which most lab courses operate, however, do not allow time of such exploration. Students dislike a failed experiment, so experiments have evolved to be as foolproof as possible and to fit into the standard once a week, 2 to 3 hours period.

Moreover, the lab course is rarely coordinated with the accompanying lecture course. The lab rarely attracts the attention of the professor who gives the lectures.

A viable alternative uses web-based applications integrated with class notes to present key scientific ideas in their historic and experimental context.

The virtual laboratory frees us from the constraints of time, resources and technique.

We can reproduce any biologic system we wish to study, from a migrating amoebae to an evolving ecosystem.

We can explore how science is really done by working scientists. virtuallyBiology™ weblabs enable us to explore what works and what does not, and why.

If you are planning to go into science or medicine as a profession, you will lose nothing in the virtuallyBiology™ laboratory. But you will benefit greatly by getting into a working laboratory as soon as possible.

No professor will really get to know you in a classroom, large or small, as well as they will when you work with them day by day. It is an experience that can open many doors.

Perhaps the most fun I ever had, and the best investment I ever made, was working as an independent study student with Wally Snipes and Alec Keith at Penn State.

You will learn a lot, and perhaps most importantly you will learn whether a career in scientific research is right for you.

How do you arrange independent study? Independent study requires a serious time commitment on your part. Plan to start early (if you are college student senior year is almost too late).

Look at the faculty's research interests and find someone working in an area you are interested in. Then go talk with them.

But remember, the University does not pay for the costs of independent study (supplies and such). Those costs are paid by independent research grants and a professor will expect you to take the opportunity they offer seriously.

You can work on independent study projects throughout your undergraduate career taking courses (e.g. MCDB 2840 and 4840), earning an honors thesis and even some money through the URAP and UROP programs.

The earlier you start, the more you will get out of it. And you will find that compared to real science, a conventional laboratory course is more virtual than virtuallyBiology™.
