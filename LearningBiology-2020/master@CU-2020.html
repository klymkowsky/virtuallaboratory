The role of assessment in course design: How do we know what students know (and are able to do)?

What are the limitations and affordances of various types assessments? 

How do we construct meaningful assessments.  How do we modify courses in the light of assessment results
  • Read McClymer & Knowles:  Ersatz Learning, inauthentic testing
    • What are tests for (learning or sorting)? What makes a test inauthentic? 
    • What are the best ways to evaluate learning?)
  • Read : Klymkowsky & Garvin-Doxas. 2020. Concept Inventories: Design: Application, Uses, Limitations & Next Steps
    • What are the key features of a Concept Test? 
    • What features are particularly sensitive when considering test outcomes? 
    • How might you use concept test outcomes data in your teaching?
  • Consider: Biology Concept Instrument (BCI) questions.
    • Which ones seems problematic to you. How would you rewrite the question?
  • Read : Trujillo et al. 2012: Using graph-based assessments within socratic tutorials to reveal and refine students' analytical thinking about molecular networks  
    • Consider the types of learning situations in which drawings are useful.  Develop a simple graphic + text answered question. Outline what you expect students graphs/drawings to reveal that might be missed in a word-only response.  
    • Looking at this paper (Williams et al 2015. "Are Noncovalent Interactions an Achilles Heel in Chemistry Education? A Comparison of Instructional Approaches." Journal of Chemical Education 92: 1979 might be helpful. 
  • In class we will generate some beSocratic-type activities

optional: Stowe & Cooper 2019. Assessment in Chemistry Education

What makes evolutionary theory difficult to accept? 

Consider Donovan's claim that conventional teaching of genetics can reinforce racist ideation (and how to avoid it).

How do we introduce the ubiquity of stochastic processes, central to the behavior of biological systems, effectively?

 

 
  • Read : Revisiting the eclipse of Darwinism
  • & Why Don’t People Think Evolution Is True? Implications for Teaching, In and Out of the Classroom 
    • What parts of Darwin's Theory of Evolution were readily accepted (and acceptable), which were not, and why?
      • consider both scientific and non-scientific reasons
      • What types of obstacles keep more "modern" people from accepting the reality of evolution?  
        • Are they the same as those of the original scientific "rejectors"?
        • Consider both the positive and negative social implications of "evolutionary thinking". 
  • Read: Garvin-Doxas & Klymkowsky, 2008 Understanding Randomness and its Impact on Student Learning
  • Small numbers of big molecules
  • ‘Noise’ to the Debate 
    • What, in your words are the differences between truly random and stochastic (noisy) processes?  Can you give an example of a truly random process?
    • What are the obstacles that keep people from understanding stochastic  processes?  
      • Is belief in magic or religion a way to control (make sense of) stochasticity? 
    • Are there advantages associated with using stochastic processes in biological systems? 
  • Read: Avoiding unrecognized racist implications arising from teaching genetics
  • & Donovan et al (2019) Toward a more humane genetics education: Learning about the social and quantitative complexities of human genetic variation research could reduce racial bias in adolescent and adult populations
    • Explain where the idea of races came from and whether you consider races real or not?  
    • Speculate on the extra-scientific implications of assuming races are real.  
    • How might teaching human genetic variation influence racist ideologies or presumptions?  
    • In preparation for group discussion:
      • How would you present the genetic bases of phenotypic variation between human populations?   
      • Speculate on the pros and cons of using the terms genes or alleles when comparing people or populations of people.

Understanding genes and where they come from.

How do we teach about the molecular mechanisms within complex non-linear systems? 
  • Read: Genes – way weirder than you thought
    • Speculate on how a Mendelian perspective clarifies and obscures the realities of genetic mechanisms.
    • Speculate on how the path to the discovery of the physical nature of the gene influenced common ideas of what genes are, where they come from, and how they act.
    • How would you define a gene?  
    • How would you use that definition as the basis for deciding how many genes are present within a specific region of a DNA molecule? 
  • Read: Schlotter (2015) Genes from scratch - the evolutionary fate of de novo genes - Trends in Genetics. 
    • How would you go about identifying a possible "de novo" gene?
    • How does the origin of gene influence its fate?
    • Does the appearance of genes de novo influence the intelligent design argument
  • Read: Conceptual simplicity and mechanistic complexity: the implications of un-intelligent design & Your Cancer Targets may not be real - Lowe. 2019
    • In the context of teaching what level of molecular detail is necessary and when does it obscure understanding?
      • illustrate with a particular example
