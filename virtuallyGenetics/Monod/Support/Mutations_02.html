Making mutations in bacteria - part 2

 

Start by growing up an overnight culture of E. coli in LB to ~10^9 cells/ml.

In the morning, dilute the cultures into fresh LB and grown to a density of ~10^8 cells/ml.

Components in LB absorb UV light, and so reduce the effective dose of radiation.

We also want to spread the bacteria out so that they do not escape exposure to radiation by hiding behind others.

To accomplish both goals, we will 'wash' the bacteria.

Sediment the bacteria out of solution using a short centrifugation. Remove the supernatant and resuspend the cells in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS).

The bacteria can survive for a limited time in PBS and PBS does not absorb UV light.

This "washing" step may be done twice to insure that the LB is completely removed.

To insure that the bacteria are uniformly exposed to the light, we will place 2-5 ml aliquots into glass petri dishes; the plates themselves sit on a rotating platform.

Based on your estimate of UV flux, expose the cells so that 25%, 10%, and 1% of the cells survive.

After the mutagenesis take 1.0 ml of mutagenized cells from each petri dish, add 10 ml of LB, and grow up overnight. Generate a separate overnight culture from each petri dish.

The next morning prepare 1:10 and 1:100 dilutions of the culture. Spread 0.1 ml of each (original, 1:10, and 1:100 dilutions) onto rifampin-containing LB agar plates.

Also, prepare a second dilution series, and determine the total number of bacteria/ml by plating onto standard LB agar plates.
