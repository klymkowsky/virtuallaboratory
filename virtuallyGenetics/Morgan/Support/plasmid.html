Introduction to plasmids & the F-plasmid specifically

 

Plasmids are typically circular DNA molecules, which range from one to many hundreds of kilobase pairs (kbps). By definition they are not essential (if they were essential of bacterial survival they would be considered chromosomes).

Plasmids contain the genetic elements required for their maintenaince in the cell. There are low and high copy plasmids, which differ in the number of copies were cell. There can be as many as 20 or more copies of a high copy plasmid in a cell, and as few as 1 copy of a low copy plasmid.

In addition to the genetic elements required for their maintanence, plasmids can also carry genes that enhance the survival or pathogenicity of the host bacterium.

 

The flexibiltiy of plasmids, in terms of size, has made them extremely useful to microorganisms in the natural world, and to research scientists in the human world. Plasmids can carry the genes responsible for antibiotic resistance. Moreover, plasmid DNA can be picked up by cells through a process known as transformation. This enables genetic traits to be transferred between unrelated organisms -- a process known as horizontal gene transfer.

(The fact that you share genes with your parents and your children is an example of vertical gene transfer).

Scientists have re-engineered plasmids to force bacterial cells to make specific proteins. The evolutionary relatedness of all organisms is reflected in the fact that genes from organisms as distinct as the potato and the whale can be transcribed and translated into the correct proteins bacterial and archeal cells (an amazing fact, if you think about it!).
