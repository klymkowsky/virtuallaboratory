Making mutations in bacteria

 

There are a number of ways to induce mutations. In our lab we will use exposure to ultraviolet (UV) light.

UV mutagenesis requires the cells contain an "SOS" repair system, which E. coli does.

Curious as to how the SOS system works?
ask your vTA

We will use the appearance of mutations for resistance to the antibiotic rifampin (100 µg/ml) as a measure of whether mutagenesis has been successful.

We will carry out our mutagenesis in two steps.

First we will define the conditions for irradiation.

Then we will use these conditions to isolated the mutant phenotypes we are interested in.

We will use a standard 15W "germicidal" lamp, which can be purchased from most electrical supply companies.

We will set up the bulb so that it is about 25-30 cm above the top of a rotating platform. There is shielding between us and the lamp, so that we are not exposed to direct or reflected UV light.

We also have an SOS-like system, and exposure to UV light generates mutations in our cells as well -- which is to be avoided.

To determine the UV flux of our system, we will use a bioassay (we could also use a more expensive "flux meter"). The dose is determined by the output of the UV lamp (how many UV photons per area per sec) multiplied by the time of exposure.

Based on many studies, it is known that a UV flux of 8J/m^2 will produce a 10% reduction in the titre of a phage T4 stock, while a dose of 50J/m^2 will reduce the viability of E. coli by 60%.
