The Microtubule Cytoskeleton

Microtubules are composed of the protein tubulin.

Tubulin is a dimer of and -tubulin polypeptides.

Tubulin binds two guanine nucleotides.

One sits at the interface between the and subunits and is an integral and stable component of the tubulin protein.

The other guanine nucleotide sits at the + end of the -tubulin subunit.

Again, as in the case of actin filaments, the microtubule's + end is the end that grows faster under polymerization conditions.

This guanine nucleotide is exchangeable. The tubulin protein can bind a GTP at this site and then catalyze its hydrolysis. It is a GTPase.

  
 

The tubulins of eukaryotic cells have a similar three dimensional structure to that of the septum forming protein FtsZ of bacteria.

Like eukaryotic tubulin, FtsZ binds guanine nucleotides and has GTPase activity. Whether FtsZ is an analog or a homolog of tubulin remains unclear.

Following translation, the and tubulin polypeptides interact with chaperones and are then assembled into tubulin proteins.

About 50% of the tubulin in a cell is soluble tubulin, about 50% is assembled into microtubules

Tubulin subunits assemble into a head to tail fashion to form parallel protofilaments.

The trickiest aspect of cytoskeletal polymer assembly is the initiation of a new polymer.

This process of nucleation requires that a number of polypeptides combine to form a stable "seed". By its very nature this is an unlikely event.

 
 
 

To facilitate and regulate tubulin assembly, the cell uses a specific structure, the microtubule organizing center or MTOC.

MTOCs are generally organized into a structure known as the centrosome.

Typically, the centrosome is located adjacent to the nucleus. It plays an key role in both interphase microtubule organization and mitosis.

MTOCs are composed a large number of polypeptides, including the tubulin-like polypeptide-tubulin.

-tubulin together with a number of other polypeptides form a structure that nucleates microtubule assembly.

Microtubules generally have their - ends buried in the MTOC, their + ends extending out into the cytoplasm.

  

Dynamic Instability

In the same way that actin is an ATPase, tubulin is a GTPase.
 

The rate of tubulin-mediated GTP-hydrolysis increases when tubulin is assembled into a microtubule.

The longer a tubulin-GTP is in the polymer, the more likely that its bound GTP will have hydrolyzed to form GDP and a phosphate.

This hydrolyzed GDP cannot be exchanged until the polymer disassembles.

Now things finally start getting complex.

  
 
The two ends of the microtubule are different, which leads to treadmilling.
 
 

Each end can also exist in two structurally different states. A GTP-tubulin capped state and a GDP-tubulin state.

The assembly dynamics of these two states are quite different.

At the concentration of free tubulin within the cell, a GTP-tubulin capped end grows; the GDP-tubulin end depolymerizes,

If tubulin-mediated GTP hydrolysis catches up with the growing end of a microtubule, that end will switch from growing to disassembling.

This behavior is known as dynamic instability.

The dynamic instability of microtubules is exploited in a number cellular processes.

It enables microtubules to "explore"" cellular space. Microtubules grow out from the centrosome.

If they interact with structures that inhibit "+" end depolymerization they are stabilized.

If not the microtubule depolymerize.

The liberated tubulin protein is used to grow new microtubules.

  
 
This generates a kind of cellular coordinate system, centered on the centrosome. This system can be used to integrate information from the cell periphery.
 
 

During interphase, the period between mitoses, microtubules are present throughout the cytoplasm.

interphase

Their organization differs depending on the cell type.

As the cell enters mitosis or meiosis, the microtubule system undergoes a profound reorganization to form a spindle.

mitosis
 

As the cell passes start and enters S phase, the centrosome duplicates.

The two centrosomes remain close to one another until the start of mitosis.

separated centrosomes

As the cell prepares to enter M phase, the centrosomes move apart. By the time the chromosomes condense and the nuclear envelope breaks down, the two centrosomes are on opposite sides of the cell.

Nuclear envelope breakdown enables microtubules to invade the nuclear space.

Early in mitosis and meiosis, the replicated chromosomes are held together in the region of their centromeres, a DNA sequence and its associated polypeptides .

Each centromere is associated with a specialized microtubule capture machine, the kinetochore.

chromosome alignment on spindle

Microtubules that interact with kinetochores or with microtubules coming from the opposite centrosome or spindle pole, are stabilized.

This process leads to the alignment of chromosomes between the two spindle poles.

The centromeric link between chromosomes
is severed at the

end of metaphase and the chromosomes walk along their attached microtubules toward the spindle poles.

This poleward movement of chromosomes involves controlled microtubule disassembly.

During disassembly, kinetochore microtubules remain attached to both kinetochores and spindle poles.

This attachment is mediated in part by microtubule-motor molecules.

They fall into two polypeptide superfamilies, the kinesins and the dyneins.

 
 
As in the case of myosin and actin, these microtubule-based motors can walk along microtubules in an ATP hydrolysis-dependent manner.
 

 

We will describe the structurally simpler kinesins,but the dyneins are conceptually similar.

Most but not all kinesins are "+" end directed motors. They move toward the "+" of the microtubule.

 

Dyneins are "-" directed motors. Both types of molecules are "bi-functional".

Their tail bind cargo. This can be membraneous vesicles, other microtubules, the kinetochore, or RNA/protein complexes.

 

This is a diagram of a standard kinesin. It is a multimeric protein composed of two heavy chain polypeptides and a number of accessory polypeptides.

 
 

It has two microtubule binding "head" domains, each acts as a microtubule-activated ATPase.

The kinesin walks along the microtubule, pulling its cargo along with it. As it walks keeps its 'feet' on microtubule surface, so it does not diffuse away.

Microtubule motors produce a distinctive type of movement know as saltatory movement.

Unlike Brownian movement, which is erratic, saltatory movements are straight and persistent.
 

The microtubule and microtubule motor system, together with its interactions with other cellular components, can generate and maintain cellular asymmetries.

As such it plays a key role in both cellular morphology and differentiation.
