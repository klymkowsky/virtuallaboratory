A very short history of molecular biology
The idea of a molecular biology has been intimately linked to studies of the identity of the genetic material.
 

There were a number of steps from Gregor Mendel's identification of hereditary "factors" to the discovery that genetic information is stored in the sequence of nucleotides within nucleic acids.

Working first using pus cells and later on salmon sperm, Friedrich Miescher described a new, non-protein component of nuclei, which he named nuclein.

This work was published in 1871.

Miescher originally believed that nuclein was a storage form for phosphorus within the cell.

Two biochemists, Albrecht Kossell and Phoebus Levene, further defined the nature of nuclein. Kossell demonstrated that it contained purines and pyrimidines and carbohydrates, Levene showed that the carbohydrates were either ribose in ribonucleic acid (RNA) or deoxyribose in deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA).

The role of DNA in heredity took quite awhile to be uncovered. It begins with observations on dividing cells.

Early students of cells, cytologists, used dyes to visualize the components of cells. For these stains to work, cells and tissues first ahve to be chemically stabilized or "fixed".

Once fixed, tissues were generally embedded in wax. The block of wax can then be cut into thin sections that can be viewed under the micrscope.

Different dyes were used to stain different cellular and extracellular structures.

 

In some cells the nucleus was replaced by densely stained bodies, known as chromosomes.

The appearance of chromosomes corresponded with rapid growth.

In 1887 Edouard van Beneden reported that the number of chromosomes was a constant for each species.

Different species had different numbers of chromosomes.


This type of observation suggested that Mendel's factors were localized to the chromosomes. But what were chromosomes made of?

The presence of DNA in nuclei and chromosomes was demonstrated through the use of the Feulgen reaction.

Was DNA the molecular basis of heredity or was genetic information held in some other compounds.

The path that to proving that DNA was the hereditary material began with Fredrich Griffiths.

In 1929 he was studying two closely related strains of the bacteria. One strain termed S, produced smooth colonies when grown in culture and caused pneumonia when introduced into mice.

 

The other strain, termed R, produced rough colonies when grown in culture and did not make mice sick.

Griffith co-injected heat-killed S cells together with living R cells into mice; the mice died of pneumonia. Moreover, he recovered S-type cells from these mice. No S cells were recovered from mice injected with heat-killed S cells.

Griffith concluded that genetic information present in the preparation of killed S cells could be picked up by the living R cells. He called this process transformation.
