IKiN (FALL 2011) questions
One (and only one) question each group will be presented on the final; this page is highlightable and you are free to discussion the questions and their answers with your class mates.
  • Section I: Evolutionary mechanisms
    • Explain how it is that evolutionary processes are unpredictable, but often produce functionally similar analogous structures (and behaviors).
    • Describe the evidence that suggests that all extant organisms share a common ancestor and this relates to the Cell Theory of Life
    • Explain the factors that encourage and constrain the effects of sexual selection.

  • Section II: Physiochemical ideas: 
    • In the context of our discussions, what distinguishes a spontaneous reaction from a thermodynamically favorable one, and describe how you might use coupled reactions to make a thermodynamically unfavorable reaction occur.
    • What determines the structure and behavior of the water molecule and water (as a substance), and how is the structure of water relevant to our understanding of lipid, protein, and nucleic acid structure?
    • Describe the molecular similarities and differences between aerobic respiration and photosynthesis in terms of the way energy is captured (in the form of relatively unstable bonds).

  • Section III: Molecular Biology
    • Describe the universe of effects of mutations on gene activity; indicate which might involve the regulatory region, the coding region, or both.
    • Given that most (if not all) genes have a useful or necessary function, explain how it is possible that new protein activities can arise during the course of evolution. Explain why lactose tolerance arose multiple time during human evolution, where as vitamin C independent has not.
    • A mutation leads to a protein that has normal activity but has a much shorter half life; provide a plausible scenario to explain this observation. A second mutation leads to a protein with normal activity and normal half live, but much lower intracellular concentration; what types of processes could explain this behavior.
