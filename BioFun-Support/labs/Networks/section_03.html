Programmed cell death or apoptosis occurs in many organisms. It is 'self-induced' (suicidal) death. Although it may, at first, seem counterintuitive -- the ability of individuals to kill themselves can confer a selective advantage to a population of organisms.

The importance of apoptosis was first appreciated in multicellular organisms.   The disappearance of the tadpole tail and the webbing between human fingers are dramatic examples of large-scale apoptotic processes.

You might think that programmed cell death would be restricted to multicellular organisms, where a part is sacrificed to achieve the goals of the whole.

You might even argue that programmed cell death would be an evolutionary impossibility for unicellular organisms such as bacteria. But the reality is more complex and surprising.

 

Bacteria rarely live in isolation and the advantages of apoptosis are understood when bacteria are considered as social organisms.   Bacteria, like all other organisms, have pathogens -- the most common of which are the bacteriophages.

Bacteria divide by fission; each of the two daughter cells are very nearly identical to their parent.

When a virus infects a cell, it can produce more that 200 new viruses in less time than it takes a bacterial cell to divide once.

If unchecked, a single virus particle could quickly kill all of the bacteria in a population.

There is a period, however, between the injection of the phage genome and the assembly of new infectious phage particles when the original phage particle has been disassembled and new phage have yet to be assembled -- this is known as eclipse.

If the infected bacterium kills itself during eclipse, the invading phage will be destroyed. The infection is over.

 

So, the obvious question is -- why would a bacterium "want" to do this?

Think about the ancestry of the bacterial culture. Bacteria are small and cannot travel far on their own.

Typically a bacterium's neighbors are its direct relatives -- they share very similar genotypes.

If the bacterial population is sensitive to the invading virus it should rapidly become extinct.

Now consider a bacterial population in which infected individuals can kill themselves before the invading virus can replicate.

The viral infection is halted by the self-sacrifice a few members of the population (who would have been doomed anyway).

 

The same population-based logic applies to the induction of programmed cell death responses during starvation.

When viewed as parts of a communal "super-organism" we can appreciate the evolutionary value of apoptosis cell death in bacteria and other unicellular organisms.

This way of thinking leads to a holistic Darwinism, that is complete consistent with Darwin & Wallace's original theory.

Inducing cell death:  Both ppGpp and the s-dependent stress response can lead to programmed cell death.

The mechanism of death involve what is known as addiction modules.

These systems were first recognized through their role in the maintenance of plasmids.

Further study revealed that addiction modules are present in the bacterial chromosome.

They all seem to work according to the same basic scheme.

They encode a stable toxin and an unstable "anti-toxin".

In some cases the anti-toxin binds to the toxin and inhibits it, in others an anti-sense RNA regulates toxin expression.

 

Given that addiction modules differ from one another in turns of mechanism, it is clear that they have evolved independently many times -- they are an example of convergent evolution.

This implies that they perform a highly advantageous function, so valuable that they have been reinvented repeatedly.
