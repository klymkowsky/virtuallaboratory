Real time evolution
  Responding to starvation:  The response to starvation is complex.  It involves both activity- and gene expression dependent components.

For simplicity's sake, we will focus our discussion on the creation of the "alarmone" ppGpp and the regulation of gene expression by the stringent response sigma factor S.

How do bacteria "know" that they are starving?  A number of changes occur when cells begin to run out of the raw materials.

 

A decline in amino acid concentration leads to the expression of genes involved in amino acid synthesis and a number of other effects.

 

Normally, the system that attaches amino acids to tRNAs keeps most tRNA "charged", i.e. attached to an amino acids.

If a cell is starving, however, the concentration of uncharged tRNAs increases.

Uncharged tRNAs compete with charged tRNAs for binding to the acceptor (a) site on the ribosome.
 

Binding of an uncharged tRNA to the a site blocks translation. The ribosome "stalls" until the uncharged tRNA leaves and a charged tRNA takes its place.

This delay in translation allows the relA protein (ppGpp synthase I) to interact with the ribosomal subunit protein L11.

relA/L11 catalyses the synthesis of the molecule pppGpp from ATP and GTP.  Released from the ribosome, pppGpp is processed by pppGpp 5' phosphohydrolase to form ppGpp.

ppGpp is an alarmone, it mediates many events within the cell.

For example, ppGpp binds to the ß-subunit of RNA polymerase, inhibiting rRNA and tRNA transcription.

ppGpp-medidated inhibition of the mazEF modulon can induce cell death!
Activation of the starvation modulon:  As specific amino acids are depleted from the medium, E. coli adapts by synthesizing them itself.

It recycles aspartic acid and uses it to synthesize the amino acids threonine, lysine, methionine and isoleucine.

The first steps in this pathway are mediated by the thrA, metL, and lysC gene products.  These enzymes are normally inhibited by threonine, methionine, or lysine, respectively.

When active, these enzymes use ATP hydrolysis to produce aspartyl phosphate, which in turn is converted into aspartate semialdehyde by the asd protein.

 

The thrA protein converts aspartate semialdehyde into homoserine.

To make threonine, homoserine phosphorylated by the thrB protein (a protein kinase), and then converted into threonine by the thrC gene product.

Both homoserine and homoserine phosphate can be converted into the alarmone homoserine lactone.

 

Homoserine lactone can be acetylated and secreted from cells, where it is used as a quorum sensor -- enabling individual bacterium to determine the density and metabolic 'well-being' of their neighbors.

Quorum sensing is used to regulate some of the"communal" properties of bacteria.

For example, the marine bacteria Vibrio harveyi uses a quorum sensing system to regulate the production of light(through a luciferase/ATP hydrolysis enzyme system.

Only when the cells are at high density is this system activated.
Inside the cell, homoserine lactone acts as a strong inducer of the spoS gene, which encodes the sigma factorS.
 

In eubacteria, factors play a critical role in the coordination of gene expression.

factors are components of the bacterial RNA polymerase (RNP) and are required to recognize regulatory sequences in specific promoters.

Different factors recognize different regulatory elements, so the expression of a particular factor coordinates the expression of entire ensemble of genes -- known as either a 'modulon' or a 'regulon'.
 
The genes regulated by s are involved in adapting the cell to starvation and environmental stress.
