Biological adaptation

Organisms rarely live in a constant environment.  There are often times of feast and times of famine, changing numbers of predators and prey, changing physical and chemical conditions.

The very process of growth changes the environment -- nutrients are used up, waste products released. One organism's waste becomes another's food or poison.

To survive, most organisms need to be able to adapt to changes in its environment.  The ability to adapt is preprogrammed into the organism.

It does not require genetic change, just a change in the activity of genes and proteins.

 

A dramatic example of this type of adaptation is the process of "chemotaxis", i.e. the ability to move toward nutrients (positive chemotaxis), and away from toxins (negative chemotaxis).

This process is controlled by regulating the activity of proteins already present in the cell.

We refer to such processes as "activity-based adaptation".

 

A second type of adaptation takes longer because it involves changes in gene expression, not just protein activity.

Genes encode either messenger RNAs (mRNAs) or structural/regulatory RNAs. mRNAs direct the synthesis of polypeptides.   Other types of RNAs play other roles within the cell.

Gene expression-based adaptation takes longer than activity-based adaptation because specific genes must be recognized by transcription factors, RNAs must be transcribed by DNA-dependent RNA polymerase, mRNAs must be translated by the ribosomal machinery, folded and assembled into functional proteins. 

Similarly, other types of RNAs generally must associated with polypeptides (as in the formation of a ribosome or a spliceosome) to be functional.

Bacteria and the study of adaptation:  Bacteria make an almost perfect system in which to study the process of adaptation. We can easily grow up cultures of identical organisms within a few hours.

In these cultures, growth stops as nutrients are exhausted. This represents a drastic change in the environment.

REVIEW ON GROWTH

We will use E. coli to study how environmental changes leads to activity- and gene expression-based adaptation.

As E. coli enter stationary phase the cells become smaller, spherical and tend to clump together.

 

Starvation-induced "mini-cells" are more resistant to antibiotics and other toxic substances, metabolically less active, and more efficient at taking up DNA from their environment (transformation).

Some marine bacteria form "ultramicrocells" with diameters ~300 nm, not much larger than the largest bacterial viruses.

The most drastic example of this process is spore formation.

 
 

 

 

Spores formed by bacteria are a specific adaptation for survival during hostile conditions.

Not all types of bacteria can form true spores.

Bacteria of the genus Bacillus commonly form spores. Spores of Bacillus anthracis can persist for decades in an dormant state.

There has been a report that spores millions of years old have been revived.
