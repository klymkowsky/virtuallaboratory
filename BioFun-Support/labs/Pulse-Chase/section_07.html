Movement through membranes

There is a relatively simple apparatus that we can use to generate such a bilayer.

A thin piece of teflon with a hole in it.

Teflon is a carbon-fluorine polymer with molecular weights as high as 30,000,000 daltons.

 

While C-F bonds are highly polar the symmetrical arrangement of fluorine (green) leads to the cancellation of charge and an almost perfectly non-polar molecular surface.

If we move the teflon slide through a surface lipid film, the hydrophobic parts of the lipids will associate with the teflon surface.

As the hole passes through the surface, a lipid bilayer will form.
 

We can use the vScope to look at how the presence of the membrane effects diffusion.

We are taking a somewhat less realistic model here, since we could not really see the movement of individual molecules (as opposed to the particles we were looking at before).

However, the principle is the same.

Using the vScope, track the movement of molecules that have been placed to one side of the bilayer.

The temperature of the system is a constant, but you now you can alter the hydrophilicity of the molecules under study. This property ranges from completely water soluble to very insoluble in water.

The goal of this part of this laboratory is to use the vScope to study how the hydrophilicity of a molecule alters its movement through a lipid membrane.

  • Describe how a highly water soluble molecule moves through the membrane. Is diffusion delayed by the presence of the membrane?
  • How does decreasing hydrophilicity alter the rate at which the molecule passes through the membrane? Is there a point were decreasing hydrophilicity leads to a change in behavior of the molecule?
