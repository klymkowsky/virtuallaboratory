Water can, in fact, exist as a liquid at 0.00°C and even lower temperatures. This is known as supercooled water. Supercooled liquid water is not stable, however. A loud noise or a thump can induce freezing.

When water goes from a liquid to a solid at 0.00°C it changes density dramatically -- it decreases from 0.9999 g/ml to ~0.93 g/ml.

What happens then? Density is mass divided by volume. Since the mass of water does not change with temperature, the volume must increase.

In our closed system, such an increase in volume would shatter the apparatus.

Solids, liquids and gases

What are the differences between a gas, a liquid and a solid? What is going on as a substance changes from liquid to solid?

A solid has a distinct shape and defined boundaries that remain unchanged over time. It resists intrusion -- as you will experience if you run into something solid.

A gas, in contrast, has no defined surface and is quite tenuous -- you can easy walk through it, you do it all the time.

A liquid occupies a position between the two. It has a distinct surface, but no defined shape -- it assumes the shape of whatever container it is placed in. You can push your hand into a liquid, and it moves out of the way.

An atomic view

Liquids are liquid because the molecules are freer to move with respect to their neighbors.

They remain coherent (liquid not gaseous) because the molecules are attracted to their neighbors.

As the temperature rises, the average speed at which the molecules are moving increases, until it becomes sufficient to overcome the interactions between molecules.

The liquid is transformed into a gas.

In a gas there is very little if any interaction between molecules.

So based on this simple view, you might predict that
All liquid forms of a substance are less dense than the solid.
 All liquid forms are more dense that the solid
 Just impossible to predict
 

In fact, in the case of water the problem is complicated by the fact that solid water (ice) can exist in a number of forms.

These exist under different conditions of pressure and temperature.

The stronger the interactions between molecules, the higher the temperature has to be before a liquid is transformed into a gas.

In the case of water, the strong H-bond interactions between molecules lead to very high temperatures for melting (solid to liquid)(0°C) and boiling (liquid to gaseous)(100°C) transitions for a molecule of its size.
