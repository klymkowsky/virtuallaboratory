Barriers to diffusion

Water's ability to form hydrogen bonds has profound effects on its properties and its interactions with other molecules.

In liquid water, individual molecules constantly interact with one another through H-bonds.

These interactions are responsible for the surface tension displayed by water.

To walk on water animals, like the water strider, use hydrophobic feet.

The molecular surface of these feet can not form H-bonds .

 

Consider what happens when a molecule dissolves in water.

We will refer to water as the solvent and compound we are trying to dissolve in the water as the solute.

Water molecules (blue) form a shell around the solute, which in this case is a protein.

If the solute can make H-bonds with water, it acts very much like water and there is no great disruption in water structure.

The solute is soluble.

 

What is if solute cannot make H-bonds?

Water molecules that happen to point one of their H-bonding groups toward the solute loose the opportunity to form an H-bond.

More importantly, to maximize the number of H-bonds that they can form, the water molecules become organized into a shell around the solute.

The system is more organized than it normally would be, entropy decreases!

This is an energetically unfavorable situation, a situation to be avoided.Molecules that cannot make H-bonds are hydrophobic, they are insoluble in water.

Conversely, molecules that make H-bonds, like water, are insoluble in hydrophobic solvents.

Cells use hydrophobic molecules to build barriers - known as membranes - to regulate the flow of hydrophilic materials into and out of the cell.

 

Membranes are build of a special type of molecule, which is termed amphipathic.

Amphipathic molecules have both hydrophilic (water-loving) and hydrophobic (water-hating) domains.

When disperse in water, amphipathic molecules will self-assemble structures in which their hydrophilic portions are in contact with water while their hydrophobic parts are out of contact with water.

 
 

A micelle is an example of such a spontaneously forming structure.

If spread on the surface of water, amphipathic molecules will arrange themselves so that their hydrophilic domains are buried in the water, while their hydrophobic domains are excluded and project into the air.

Under some conditions, these layers can double over themselves to form a bilayer.

In biological systems, amphipathic lipids are used to build bilayers.
