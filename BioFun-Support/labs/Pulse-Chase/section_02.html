Collecting data and calculating half-lives

Once the pulse is completed, and the chase begins, we need to determine the amount of radioactivity in the specific protein we are interested in.  

The time it takes for the radioactivity associated with a protein to fall its orignal amount is the half-life of the newly synthesized protein.  

One common need some specific way of counting the radioactivity in the protein we are interested and ignorning the the radioactivity that has been  incorporated into all of the other proteins present (and synthesized) in the cell during the pulse period. 

typically, this is done with antibodies, these are immune system proteins that will bind to specific target proteins.

One nice thing about pulse-chase experiments is that the same basic strategy can be used in many different systems.  

What you need is a way to specifically label a particular population at a particular time, and then you need a way to follow that labelled population over time. 

 
 
  • You label a cell with 14C-leucine or 35S-methionine; while both proteins are labelled to a similar extent with 14C, one of the two proteins is labelled much more strongly with 35S.  How is that possible?
  • You add a radioactive amino acid to a culture of cells, what factors determine the time and extent to which radioactivity is found in proteins?
