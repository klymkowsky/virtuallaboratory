Working with populations

It is often the case that we are interested in determining the life-time of a protein, or how it moves through a cell.  Similarly, we might want to know when a cell is "born" and what happens to it over time. 

Answering such questions can be technically difficult; it may be impractical or impossible to follow individual molecules or to look at the same cell over time. 

How do we get the answers we want to such questions?  A commonly used approach is the "pulse-chase" experiment. 

One nice thing about pulse-chase experiments is that the same basic strategy can be used in many different systems.  

What you need is a way to specifically label a particular population at a particlar time, and then you need a way to follow that labelled population over time.

We will first look at how pulse chase experiments can be used to determine the stability or "half-life" of a specific protein.   In other parts of this lab, we will (eventually) look at the movement of newly synthesized proteins through the cell and the birth and fate of cells within tissues. 

Proteins are constantly being synthesized and degraded. 

To label newly synthesized proteins, we add to radioactive forms of amino acids – typically these contain either 14C or 3H or 35S atoms.  These are incorporated into newly synthesized proteins, making them radioactive as well.    This is the pulse.

 
 

To stop the labeling of newly synthesized proteins, we remove unincorporated radioactive label from the system this case. 

Even so, some radioactive label will remain – to reduce its incorporation into newly synthesized protein, we add a large excess of a non-radioactive (or cold) version of the radioactive marker.   This is the chase.

Each radioactive compound has a specific activity, determined by the number of radioactive atoms within it.  When we add large amounts of the non-radioactive molecule, it effectively reduced the specific activity of the labelling compound.
 
  • You label a cell with 14C-leucine or 35S-methionine; while both proteins are labelled to a similar extent with 14C, one of the two proteins is labelled much more strongly with 35S.  How is that possible?
  • You add a radioactive amino acid to a culture of cells, what factors determine the time and extent to which radioactivity is found in proteins?
