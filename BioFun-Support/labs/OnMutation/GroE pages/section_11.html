Phage-resistant mutations: the chaperone GroE.

Most phage-resistant bacterial mutations fail to bind phage.

Some bind phage, however, but fail to support their growth. -- What are the nature of these mutations?

Even the most complex phage rely almost completely on their hosts for the functions required for replication. A mutation in the host that compromises, but do not abolish these functions will disrupt phage replication.

Simply slowing down phage assembly can enable an infected cell to kill itself before phage assembly has occured, leading to the elimination of the phage.

In the absence of phage, such host mutations would be selected against, since they compromise a needed host gene function.

In the presence of phage, however, the same mutations can provide an extremely strong selective advantage - why? - because it better to have a slight disadvantage in replication than to be dead.

The advantage or disadvantage associated with a particular allele (or more broadly a particular genotype) is clearly a function of the organism's environment.

We will examine (very briefly) one such class of mutations, those that alter the activity of the protein chaperone GroE.

Chaperones play a critical role in protein folding and renaturation. There are two general classes of chaperones.

Group I chaperones, such as GroE, are found in the cytoplasm of eubacterial cells and within the mitochondria and chloroplasts of eukaryotic cells.

Group II chaperones are found in the cytoplasm of eukaryotes and archeal cells.

 Supports

As polypeptides are synthesized they fold into their native, functional state.

Folding is determined by the primary sequence of the polypeptide.

But proteins are dynamic structures -- in part because their functional or "native" states are only slightly more stable than the unfolded states.

In fact there are cases when the functional state is not the most stable, thermodynamically.

In the cell, GroE acts to maintain proteins in their native configuration by catalyzing the refolding of partially unfolded polypeptides.

In solution, proteins are bombarded by other molecules.

As the temperature increases, the intensity of this bombardment also increases -- and proteins are increasingly at risk unfolding.

To protect itself, the cell respond to increasing temperatures by synthesizing "heat shock proteins", many for which are chaperones - like GroE.

The chaperones re-fold proteins unfolded in response to increased temperature.

If GroE does not function at normal efficiency few or no phage are assembled.

GroE is composed to two subunits, GroEL and GroES. Only mutations in GroEL effect phage T4 growth.

Why? because T4 encodes its own GroES-like polypeptide.

Why would host chaperones be necessary during phage assembly?
Because the phage needs to make, and correctly fold, many copies of the polypeptides involved in the assembly of new virions very quickly.
Because an inappropriately folded polypeptide could disrupt virion assembly, reducing the total number of infectious phage particles made.
Because certain steps in the assembly of a complex structure such as phage might require accessory factors, chaperones, to occur properly.
Because the phage uses the ATP synthesized by the chaperone to power its assembly processes.
