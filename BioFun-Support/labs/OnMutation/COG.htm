T-even phage behave differently from many other phage, in that there is a strong "lysis inhibition" associated with infection by multiple phage.

This has an effect on plaque morphology - wild type produce plaques with "fuzzy" boundaries. Why? because at the edges of the plaque, many bacteria are infected by multiple phage, which inhibits their lysis.

The infected cell still dies, and virions are still produced, but lysis does not occur efficiently, and when it does occur it can take quite some time.

This lysis inhibition is eliminated by mutations designated "r". Mutations in the "r" genes (referred to as r-) can be identified through their effects on plaque morphology. r- mutant phage produce clear plaques that are larger and have sharp edges.

All of the T4 phage we are using in our experiments carry mutations in r.

Seymour Benzer's analysis of mutations in the r region were critical to our understanding of the nature of genes.
