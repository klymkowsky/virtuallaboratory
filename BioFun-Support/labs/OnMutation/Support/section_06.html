Fighting phage infection in E. coli

As you may have expected, bacteria have evolved a number of strategies to deal with phage infection.

One is based upon the the close "familial" relationships between the bacteria in a culture or a small region of the environment -- they are very likely to be descended from the same ancestor and therefore share very similar genotypes -- they are clonally and closely related to one another.

Under these conditions, adaptations that benefit the population, even at a severe cost to an individual, can be selected for.  This is known as "inclusive fitness" or "kin selection".

 

 

There is a period in the phage life cycle known as eclipse.

During eclipse no active phage are present within an infected bacterium.

If the bacterium were to die during eclipse, the infecting phage would be destroyed as well.

The bacterium's neighbors would be spared.

 

Bacteria have a number of mechanisms by which to perform programmed cell death or apoptosis (pronouncing apoptosis).

Typically they use a long-lived protein toxin. This toxic protein is made constantly by the bacterium.  Left on its own, the toxin would kill the bacterial cell.

 

Normally, however, the activity of the toxin is inhibited by an unstable, short-lived "antitoxin".

As long as the antitoxin is actively synthesized, the cell survives.

An early event in the infectious cycle of many virulent phage is the inhibition of host cell gene expression (that is transcription and translation) and the destruction of the host's DNA.

Once this occurs, synthesis of the antitoxin stops. Since it is unstable, it disappears from the cell more rapidly than does the more stable toxin molecule.

As antitoxin levels drop, toxin is activated and causes cell death and phage elimination.
