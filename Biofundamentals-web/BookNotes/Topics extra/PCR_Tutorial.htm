For PCR analysis, we take a sample and extract the nucleic acids present.

Because ribosomal RNAs are so highly conserved, and yet display distinctive characteristics of each species, whether bacterial, archaeal and eukaryal, it is common to analyze ribosomal RNAs.

These RNAs are used to generate complementary or cDNA copies by a process known as reverse transcription.

The enzyme that catalyzes this process is, of course, reverse transcriptase.

 

It is used by some viruses and other self-replicating, and inherently selfish, nucleic acids to make DNA copies of RNA.

  

 

PCR was made possible the discovery of thermostable DNA-dependent, DNA polymerases.

Many such polymerases have been isolated from thermophilic and hyperthermophilic archea. These organisms are known as extremophiles!

PCR is carried out using a machine known as a thermocycler. It heats and cools reactions in an accurate and programmable manner.

We set up reactions so that they contains our target or template cDNA, two oligonucleotide primers, DNA polymerase, and deoxynucleotide triphosphates.
 

At the beginning of the reaction, the temperature is increased.

The double-stranded DNA template denatures into single strands.

The primers are short, between 15 to 20 bases long. We designed these oligos based on the sequences we wish t isolate.

 

Primers are used in pairs - one primer is directed against one strand in the DNA sequence to be amplified, the other against the complementary strand.


PCR
 

Their 3' OH ends point at one another!

The primers are present at many times the concentration of their target sequences within the original template DNA.

As the temperature decreases, the primers bind to, or anneal, to complementary sequences in the template DNA.

 
 

The higher the annealing temperature, the more specific the interaction.

Too high and no binding will occur, too low and non-specific binding will predominate.

After annealing, the temperature is increased optimize the activity of the DNA polymerase, which extends the primers, based on the template DNA sequence.
 
After a set amount of time, the polymerase can synthesis ~1000 bases of DNA per minute, the temperature is raised again to ~94 °C to denature all of the strands in the reaction.
 

When the temperature is returned to the annealing temperature, the cycle begins again.

Each cycle leads to a doubling of the number of target DNA.

The resulting amplified DNA can be isolated from the reaction mix by a process known as gel electrophoresis.

To analyze individual amplified DNA molecules, each originally copied from a specific template DNA molecule, they must be be cloned.

This is done by subcloning pieces of DNA into a plasmid.

 
 

In subcloning, pieces of DNA are inserted into the plasmid. A plasmid is basically a selfish piece of DNA. It must contain a DNA-replication origin to be propagated in a bacterial cell.

We then transform the plasmid into a bacterial host.

  Most plasmids have made themselves useful or nasty, some contain addition modules.

One way to be useful is to contain genes that encode antibiotic resistance.

If a bacterium picks up a plasmid that encodes an antibiotic resistance gene, that bacterium will become resistant to the antibiotic.

To accept a DNA insert, the plasmid must be cut -- generally using one or two restriction endonucleases; these.are part of the host cell's defense against foreign, selfish, DNAs, such as plasmids.

DNA molecules with compatible ends can insert into this opening and be linked to the plasmid DNA by a DNA ligase and ATP.

Generally the system is set up so that plasmids are cut in such a way that if no DNA is inserted the plasmid cannot be recircularized by the ligase.

Under most conditions, linear DNA molecules are not replicated in bacteria. Circular DNA molecules, containing an inserted DNA sequence, are replicated.

While the process of insertion and transformation is inefficient, we have many bacteria and a powerful selection.

 

By plating the transformed bacteria onto agar plates that contains the appropriate antibiotic, only those bacteria that contain a circularized plasmid will grow.

We select for those bacteria that grow in the presence of the antibiotic, they must contain stable plasmids. .

Each bacteria with a plasmid can grow as a clone into a visible colony. All of the cells of the clone will contain copies of the same plasmid.

We isolate the plasmid from the bacteria and sequence the inserted DNA.

This DNA was originally derived, via rtPCR, from a particular RNA present in one of the organisms present in the original biofilm.

  
 

Each type of organism contains its own characteristic ribosomal RNAs. Two distinct species contain distinct rRNAs.

The more the rRNAs of two organisms differ, the further are the two organisms in evolutionary distance.

By searching through a library of cloned sequences we can deduce the number of different organisms within the original population!

From this information, we can identify organisms and construct family trees.
