Biological diversity: 

At about the same time as the experiments that ruled out spontaneous generation as a viable theory, the European voyages of discovery (and conquest) were occurring. 

As naturalists explored the world, many creatures previously thought to be real failed to be found, while more and more different organisms were discovered and described.

It is currently estimated that there are between 1 to 2 (or more) million distinct species of organisms on earth.

Estimates based on the fossil record suggest many more of organisms have existed on the earth in the past. 

Many live in extremely restricted environments. 

Most creation stories do not even hint at this level of biological diversity. 

From a scientific perspective it raises an important question, where did all of these organisms come from? 

And is there a natural process that can explain biological diversity?

 
 

There have been a number of schemes for classifying the relationships between organisms.

Originally three "kingdoms", plants, animals and single-celled monera were proposed

Later, a five kingdom scheme of animals, plants, fungi, protozoa, and bacteria was popular, and can be still found in some textbooks (and on the web).

In the light of a growing body of molecular data, however, a new three kingdom model has emerged, the Archaea, the Bacteria and the Eukarya.

Plants, animals, fungi and protozoa are all members of the Eukarya.

  


This is the current tree of life. The exact pattern of its branches changes as new data and new insights lead to revisions and reinterpretations.

For example, among the Bacteria and Archaea, it is possible for organisms to pick up genes from other organisms, a process known as horizontal gene transfer. 

HGT is responsible for the rapid spread of antibiotic resistance in modern bacteria and probably played an key roll in the early evolution of life – at the same time, it can make establishing unambiguous phylogenic relationships difficult. 

At the root of the tree of life is the common unit of life, the cell.

The basic and universal features of cells were firmly established in the last common ancestor of all life on earth. 

Cells are bounded by a plasma membrane that controls the movement of molecules into and out of the cytoplasm.

The cytoplasm contains the molecular machinery needed for the ongoing processes of life. To fuel these processes, the cell contains systems to capture chemical and light energy from the external world. 

In bacteria and the archaea the genetic material, the genome (composed of DNA), is located within the cytoplasm. 

It contains the information needed to build a new cell and to respond to environmental changes.

 

Bacteria and archaea were originally lumped together as prokaryotes, meaning before or without a nucleus.  Both are microscopic organisms, although they can grow into communities, known as biofilms, that are easily visible.

For a long time, the archaea were overlooked (or classed with the bacteria) because they generally grow in rather extreme environments, such as hot springs – they can be difficult to grow in the lab.

From a gross structural view bacteria and archaea can be difficult to distinguish. Molecular studies, however, reveal that they are no more closely related to one another than they are to organisms with a nucleus, the eukarya. 

 

Most of the macroscopic (that is large and visible to the naked eye) organisms we are aware of, the plants, animals and fungi, are eukarya.

The defining feature of the eukarya is a distinct, double membrane-bounded organelle, the nucleus, located within the cytoplasm of the cell.

The bulk of the cell's genetic material lies within the nucleus.

A nuclear envelope separates the nuclear interior from the cytoplasm.

The nuclear envelope is fenestrated by structurally distinctive (octagonal) nuclear pores that mediate the movement of molecules into and out of the nucleus.

Eukarya are hybrid organisms

A critical feature of eukarya, in addition to a nucleus, is the presence of cytoplasmic organelles, known as mitochondria.  It appears that mitochondria present in all eukarya. 

The possible exception are the amitochondrial eukarya, such as Giardia. 

These are obligate parasites and appear to have lost mitochondria (although there is still debate on that point). 

 

Plants contain both mitochondria and chloroplasts; both types of organelles are descended from what were once free-living organisms - bacteria in fact. 

Mitochondria are derived an α-proteobacterium, while chloroplasts appear to be derived from photosynthetic cyanobacterium.

This conclusion is based on the observation that mitochondria and chloroplasts contain chromosomes and ribosomes similar to those found in bacteria, and distinct from those found in archaea or eukarya.

Whether the event that lead to the formation of the eukarya involved the 'simple' fusion of two distinct organisms or was the end result of a more long-term endosymbiotic relationship remains unclear.

The organisms that became mitochondria and chloroplasts have, over time, become greatly simplified and have lost their ability to survive on their own. 

Most of their genetic material has been lost or transferred to the nucleus of the "host" cell.

From the ~3000 to 4000 genes in a typical bacterium, a typical mitochondrion contain less than 100 genes.

How is such a strange relationship possible? It turns out that inside a cell can be a very attractive place to live, as a number of pathogens have discovered.  The process of intracellular endosymbiosis continues today.

There are even examples in which endosymbionts contain their own endosymbionts, as well as evidence for the transfer of genes from an endosymbiont to the host cell's nucleus.

Multicellular organisms

Bacteria and archaea arose between 3.5 to 3.0 billion years ago. It took another 1.5 billion years for the eukarya to appear.

Fossils of multicellular organisms did not appear until ~650 million years ago, although microscopic multicellular organisms may have been present much earlier.

 

The earliest multicellular organisms appeared during the Vendian period, which lasted from 650 to 544 million years ago.

These are known as Vendian or Ediacarian organisms.  Exactly how they are related to modern multicellular organisms remains a subject of debate. 

It is also not known whether different "type" are actually different organisms or different developmental stages of the same organism. 

It is unclear what triggered the appearance of multicellular organisms after so many years.


The earth has gone through many dramatic changes during its history and it is possible that one of these global catastrophes produced the conditions needed to select for multicellular organisms.

In any case, by the Cambrian age, which began about 544 million years ago, a number of quite complex metazoans (multicellular animals) and metaphyta (multicellular plants) had appeared.

If you think about it, there are only a few paths that lead from unicellular to multicellular organisms.

The colonial route involves cells that either fail to separate after division or that clumped together.

 

Alternatively, an single-celled organism containing multiple nuclei, a type of cell known as a syncytium, could divide to form distinct cells.

The simplest of the known metazoans are the placozoans.

Shown here is a artist's drawing of the only known placozoan, Trichoplax adhaerens.

They are composed of at least four distinct cell types.

Placozoans were first discovered in 1883 in an aquarium and appear to define a new phlya.  No fossil placozoans are known.

It is worth remembering that while evolution implies change over time, it does necessarily mean increasing complexity.  There are many situations in which simplification can lead to a reproductive advantage.

It is possible that placozoans are degenerate, that is they are derived from more complex organisms and have evolved to reach their current, apparently primitive state.

All modern metazoans appear to share a common ancestor.

This conclusion, originally based on structural similarities, has been repeatedly confirmed by molecular analyses.


 

Metazoans share common 'signaling' and genetic pathways by which they control embryonic development.

If all metazoans have a common ancestor, can we identify the unicellular eukaryote they are most closely related to?

A number of pieces of evidence suggest that the closest unicellular ancestor to the animals are the choanoflagellates.
