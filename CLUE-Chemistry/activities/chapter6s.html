Chapter 6: Elements, bonding, and physical properties

Knowledge Statements

  1. Understand the meaning of the terms: solute, solvent, solution, solvation, molarity, concentration, dilute, concentrated, micelle, colloid, and emulsion.
  2. A solution is a stable heterogenous molecular mixture; it will not become “unmixed” over time. The factors that affect solubility are the enthalpy change (ΔH), and the entropy change (ΔS).
  3. Solubility is determined by both entropic and ethlapic factors, which is a function of both solvent and solute.
  4. The ability to calculate molarify of the solution.
  5. Be able to visual and illustrate solute-solvent interactions.

Performance expectations:

  • Perform calculations using molarity (M) and volume of solution, to convert to mass of solute, and determine volume of a dilute or concentrated solutions. Predict concentrations of ions in solution when given, for example, g/L or mol/L of solute.
  • Draw the structure of molecules in solution (include solvent).
  • Relate temperature changes to the interactions that are broken and formed during the solution process. Describe the solution process in terms of entropy changes for both solute, and solvent.
  • Draw molecular level diagrams showing how solute and solvent interact, including for example, how water molecules solvate ionic compounds. Predict which molecular compounds can form hydrogen bonds with water

  • Draw what happens when NaCl, NH4Cl, CH3OCH3, CH3CH2CH3, CH3CH2OH dissolve in water. Why is methane not very soluble in water? (make sure to consider both enthalpy and entropy effects)
  • Explain why we include temperature when reporting solubility data.
  • What structural features are necessary for a molecule to form a micelle?
  • Why doesn’t the formation of micelles (and lipid bilayer cell walls) violate the second law of thermodynamics?
    What are colloids and emulsions. How do they differ from solutions. Explain how different inclusions in metals can alter the properties of the metal. (consider steel, brass, bronze)
  • How can you determine the difference between a colloid and a solution? Why can you determine the difference?
  • Give some examples of colloids. Why is bronze harder than copper? Why is steel harder than iron?
