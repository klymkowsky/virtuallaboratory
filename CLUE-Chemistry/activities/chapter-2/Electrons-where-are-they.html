Electrons – where are they and how do we know?

In an atom, each electron can be described by quantum mechanics-based mathematical equations that produce four  quantum numbers (n, l, ml, ms ).  The first three of these numbers describes the orbital (where the electron is likely to be in space), while the fourth describes the electron’s spin.  

While you may have seen visualizations of the orbital, but what do they really mean? What is the evidence behind these pictures? What do they represent?

An important source of data comes from measuring the amount of energy it takes to remove an electron (e-) from an atom in the gas phase (g): this is known as the ionization energy.

            Atom in the gas phase A(g) + energy → A+ (g) + e-  (g)


There are several factors that determine how much energy is required to remove an electron from an atom.

As a first approximately, we can use Coulomb’s Law to predict and/or explain relative ionization energies. 

For example Coulomb’s Law tells us that the larger the (average) distance between the charges the lower the attractive or repulsive force between the charges. The size of an isolated atom is determined by the attraction between the electrons and the nucleus, and the repulsions between the electrons. So lets try and make a few simple predictions. 

Consider atom of magnesium (elemental symbol Mg) which has 12 protons and 12 electrons and an atom of calcium (elemental symbol Ca) has 20 protons and electrons.

  • Is it easier or harder to knock a second electron from a Mg+ ion?  What  factors were important for your thinking about the answer?
