It makes (some) sense that the first electron to be knocked will be the easiest to remove, that is, the one that is least attracted to the nucleus. Since the electron's attraction to the atom is a function of its charge, the rest of atom's charge, and (inversely) the distance between the electron and the rest of the nucleus.

  • Why is an electron (in an atom) attracted to the rest of the atom?  

While we don’t know exactly where electrons are, quantum mechanical calculations give us an idea about how insights.   The more electrons in an atom, the further away some will be from the nucleus and the more electrons there will be between the outer most electron and the nucleus.

This will result in a weaker interaction between the nucleus and the electron, making it easier to remove.   We might well predict that an electron from calcium (with more protons and electrons) will be easier to remove than one from magnesium (with fewer protons and electrons).  
           
Now consider what happens when we try a remove a second electron (the second ionization potential).  Once one electron has been removed, the atom (known known as an ion) will have a net positive charge. The remaining electrons feel a greater attraction (more positive charge per electron) and less repulsion (fewer electrons between the electron and the nucleus). 

We predict that any positive ion will be smaller than its parent atom because the electron clouds shrink – and are held closer to the nucleus. The closer an electron is to the nucleus – the harder it will be to remove. For Mg (g) the first ionization energy = 738 kJ/mol, while the second ionization energy = 1450 kJ/mol.

Can we generalize this observation? One prediction we might make is that atoms with more protons and electrons should increase in size, and their first and perhaps their second ionization energy should go down. So lets take a look at  magnesium and chlorine
