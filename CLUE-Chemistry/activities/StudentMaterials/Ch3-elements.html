Chapter 3: Bonding
chapter map

Atoms of elements with atomic number greater than 2 (ie bigger than He) are the product of fusion reactions in stars.

Properties of elements can be explained by the type of bonding in that element. Conversely, if you know what type of bonding is present in the element, properties can be predicted.

Properties of atoms are different from the properties of macroscopic materials.

Some elements exist as discrete molecules - such as H2, F2, O2, N2, and some as extended networks such at metals and carbon. Elements that exist as discrete molecules have lower melting and boiling points than those that exist as extended networks (which are usually solids)

The properties of the elements that exist as extended networks depend on the size of the particles. Particles that are about a nanometer tend to have different properties than those that are macroscopic because they have larger surface area to volume ratio.

When atoms form bonds, atomic orbitals interact to form molecular orbitals. the number of molecular orbitals that form is equal to the number of atomic orbitals that interact. the MOs that are lower in energy than the original AOs are bonding orbitals, and electrons in those orbitals stabilize the molecule/material, Those that are higher are antibonding MOs and electrons in these orbitals destabilize the molecule.

Electrons can move between molecular orbitals by absorbing or emitting photons.

The gap between bonding and antibonding orbitals determines some properties of materials.

Electrical conductivity stems from delocalized molecular orbitals that extend over the whole chemical species.
