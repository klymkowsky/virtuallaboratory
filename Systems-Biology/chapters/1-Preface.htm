Preface  

What is central about biological systems, from the molecular to the ecological, is that they are based on the behavior of networks of interacting "biological objects".

Biological behaviors emerge from these networks; networks that have been shaped by evolutionary processes (mutation, drift, selection).

To understand the behavior of biological systems, we need to understand network behavior. Network behavior reflex the connectedness of biological systems.

  

We need to put the network into context. Are we talking about baby and parental penguins, neurons in a brain, cells within a developing embryo, organisms in an ecosystem, or genes in a cell? These are questions about the scale and complexity of the system we are considering.

Altogether, the study of networks in biological contexts is the emerging discipline of systems biology. Systems biology is more quantitative and more analytical than standard biology, and so better suited to testing the predictions of molecular and higher order analyses.

There are already a number of well-designed courses in Systems Biology, particularly e.g. Uri Alon's series of youtube videos [here] and his book [here], but they are presented more from the perspective of a student of physics or engineering coming to biology.

This course is designed to approach systems biology from the perspective of a reasonably well prepared biology student. We will introduce analytical concepts and tools as we proceed through these systems, and this will, inevitably, require that we get more and more comfortable with the mathematical tools required.

We recommend that you have already taken a course like MATH 1310: Calculus, Systems & Modeling, but it is not completely necessary. We also assue that you have successfully completed the first four courses in the MCDB@UCBoulder curriculum.
