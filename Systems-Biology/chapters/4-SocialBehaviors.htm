4: Communities and social coordination

While it is common to think about bacteria (and microbes in general) as rugged individualists, this often much too simplistic. In fact, microbes of the same and different types often live in close proximity to one another.

You can directly experience one such microbial ecosystem if you refrain from brushing your teeth for a few days. The film that builds up on your teeth is a microbial community. [Manci et al., 2013]

  

Within these communities, there are quite complicated interactions going on. Some microbes are making toxins to limit the growth and survival of others (and avoiding the effects of those molecules on themselves). Others are cooperating metabolically with one another, using molecules synthesized and secreted by other types of organisms (and visa versa).

Often the microbes within such a community are co-dependent upon one another, which is one reason that it is difficult to grown isolated microoorganisms on their own – it is estimated that >99% of all types of microbes have yet to be cultured "alone" (axenically) in the lab [Wintemule & Silve, 2010], presumably due to their interdependent. This has made it necessary to use "shot gun sequencing" to even characterized the variety of organisms that live in a particular various environment, such as your gastrointestinal tract!

The dynamics and importance of these microbial ecosystems is just beginning to be understood. For example, within such communities, there is the transfer of genetic information (DNA molecules) from cell to cell. This process of horizontal gene transfer is of significance in terms of general microbial evolution, and in medical terms, the rapid spread of antibiotic resistance.

In many cases, the social behavior of cells is regulated by the concentration of the cells. This makes sense. If a process depends upon the cooperation of cells, and there are not enough cells around to make the process occur efficiently, it is a waste of resources to begin the process.

This process has a name, it is known a quorum sensing. Since we are trying to respond to organisms of a specific type, we will have to use some physical mechanism to recognize them. The obvious choice is a molecule that they secrete, but other organisms that we might like to ignore do not.
