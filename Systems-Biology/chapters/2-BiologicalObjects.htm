Biological objects & their interactions: Basics

When we think about the interactions between biological objects, we have to think about various complementary factors: these include i) what can each object do; ii) how do interactions between objects influence those activities and finally, iii) what role does the system of interacting objects do?

For example, consider a gene, that is a region of a DNA molecule, with a bacterial gene. The gene can be expressed (transcribed and perhaps translated), and either the transcript or the encoded protein can interact with other molecules with the cell, to produce various behaviors.

Typically, we define the regulatory regions of gene via mutations that disrupt normal expression. In modern world this has been complemented, but certainly not replaced by various interaction studies that identify where specific proteins are bound on DNA molecules and how DNA molecules are packaged with other proteins (the purview of epigenetics).

Depending upon what other molecules (typically proteins) are present and "active" within a cell, are target gene will be expressed or not. If expressed, the level of its expression (in terms of RNA transcripts) or terminal gene product (RNA or polypeptide) can be high or low. The activity of a gene product can also be regulated, in part by sequestration or activation through interactions with other molecules.
