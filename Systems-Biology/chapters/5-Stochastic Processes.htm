A brief introduction to an stochastic adaptive network (must read→ Kilfoil et al., 2009):

While much of our time will be spend examining molecular and cellular networks, it is perhaps worth considering with a range of biological systems to begin with to get comfortable with the nature of interactions and analysis methods that unlie complex biological behaviors.

For examine, consider the ant Harpognathos saltator. These animals live in small colonies containing ~50 individuals. The behavior of animals that live in colonies arises from what is known as social evolution and the concept of inclusive fitness, which we will return to since it impacts a number of systems.

A colony is typically founded by a fertile individual, a queen. The eggs they lay develop into workers, who are reproductively inactive (Peeters et al., 2000; Liebig et al., 2000).

This is an example of a a highly social species, in which different individuals in the colony are analogous to different cells in the body of multicellular organism, with the workers as the body (or soma) and the queen as the germ line.

This means that, because they are related to the queen, if their (the workers) behavior leads to significantly greater reproductive success for the queen, that increase in reproductive success can more than compensate for their own lack of direct offspring, particularly when compared to the reproductive success of individuals living in isolation or without such a "division of labor".

 

At the same time, this type of structure makes the colony vulnerable. What would happen if the queen were to die? What we find is that the colony displays an underlying network organization that enables it to adapt to this eventuality. If the queen leaves the colony, some worker's become reproductively active.

This itself leads to the question, if workers can be become queens, what normally stops them from doing so?

We can outline the majority components of this system in terms of objects (the individual ants) and their interactions with one another.

At the onset there are two sets of individuals in the system, the reproductively inactive workers and the reproductively queen. Let us assume that the favored state for an ant is to be reproductively active, and we will represent each ant (the objects in our system) as either reproductively inactive (antoff), that is a worker or reproductive active (anton), a queen.

We can think of the antoff state as unstable, such that the transition between antoff → anton occurs with some finite probability per unit time, while the reverse reaction anton → antoff essentially does not occur. Queens do not turn back into workers, rather they leave or die.

So one might expect that over time, all of the workers would become queens, and the advantage of the colony's "division of labor" would be lost. But, if we examine the normal situation we find that colonies are stable – in the presence of the queen the workers remain workers.

How do we understand the stability and adaptability of this system. If we examine the interactions between these individuals, we see two types of behavior.

Workers jump on top and hold down other workers. This activity appears to inhibit the the rate of reproductive transition. We can illustrate this interaction as:

We might even speculate that the reciprocal inhibitory interactions between workers removes some of this burden from the queen.

In any case, we can see evidence for the queen's inhibitory interactions when the queen is removed. The inhibitory interactions between workers are not sufficient to maintain their reproductively inactive state.

We know (and will illustrate repeatedly as we go on) that there are phenotypic variations between individuals that arise from a number of sources (Liebig et al, 2000), including stochastic (random) variations in gene expression between genetically identical individuals.

 

SO, not all workers are identical and, in the absense of the inhibitory queen, these differences become apparent.

Some individuals may be less sensitive to worker inhibition than others (or perhaps stronger in inhibiting others).

 

Based on these variations, a few of the workers progress to become reproductively active, they become "queens".

There appearance reestablishes a new steady state within which reproductively active queens exist with reproductively inactive workers.

We can begin to think about the interactions between individuals in terms of a network of interactions (inhibitory in this case) between objects (workers and queens).

An interesting aspect of this network is that the behavior of the objects is influenced by their personal history, which in some cases keeps them as workers and in others leads to their transition from queen to worker, where they display a distinct set of behaviors.
