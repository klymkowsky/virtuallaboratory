Knowledge statements

  • Cellular membranes are derived from pre-existing membranes.
  • Membranes grow (and shrink) by the insertion (or removal) of their structural components, primarily proteins and lipids.
  • The defining feature of cellular membranes is the lipid bilayer; lipids typically account for ~50% of the membrane by mass.
  • Lipids are not a coherent group, but are defined by the presence of discrete hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions - they are “amphipathic”.
  • The hydrophobic regions of lipids (and other molecules) interact with one another (and other molecules) through van der Waals interactions; the strength of these interactions are influenced by molecular shape and surface area.

  
  • The hydrophilic regions of lipids (and other molecules) interact with one another through H-bonds; H-bonds arise from differences in electronegativities between different types of atoms, and bond orientations.The so-called hydrophobic effect arises from entropic effects associated with interactions with water molecules.
  • All membranes are inherently asymmetric, if only because their two “faces” interact with different environments.
  • Structural asymmetries in membranes arise from the asymmetric distribution of lipids, proteins, and the intrinsic polarity of proteins and their modifications, particularly the presence of highly hydrophilic carbohydrate moieties (which are typically on the extracellular face of membranes).
  • Membrane proteins are classified as integral (require detergents to be extracted from the membrane) and peripheral (can be extracted without detergents).Integral membrane proteins have distinct regions associated with the distinct faces of the membrane in which they are embedded.
  • The association of proteins with a membrane often involves a co-translational process, involving translational pausing, membrane-receptor docking and membrane translocation.
  • Variations of this process enable a protein to leave the site of synthesis (the cytoplasm) and to pass completely through a membrane.
  • Asymmetric distribution of lipids is established and maintained by enzymatic factors that can move lipid molecules directionally from one side of the membrane to the other.
  • The structure of membranes implies an intrinsic fluidity, above a particular temperature (the melting temperature) membrane molecules can move with respect to one another unless they are physically tethered to immobile structures, such as cytoskeletal or extracellular matrix components.
  • The composition of the membrane determines its “melting temperature” (as well as its “sharpness”.

Questions to ponder

  • How does changing membrane lipid (or protein) composition influences a membrane’s melting temperature?
  • Why does the presence of cholesterol alter the melting curve of membranes?
  • How might the membrane environment influence membrane protein function?
  • What thermodynamic factors make a molecular water soluble, and their the same or different from the factors that inhibit its ability to pass through a lipid membrane?
  • Why might different membranes in a eukaryotic cell (or different types of eukaryotic cells, or different types of prokaryotic cells) differ in their composition?
  • What molecular properties are necessary for a membrane to display osmotic effects?
