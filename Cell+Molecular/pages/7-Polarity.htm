Knowledge statements

  • While there are often many types of cells, particularly in the context of metazoan organisms, they can be roughly divided into epithelial, mesenchymal, and those cells found in the blood or lymphatic fluids.
  • In the simplest case, epithelial cells are coupled to one another to form a sheet; the “basal” surface of that sheet interacts with specialized extracellular matrix (often known as a basal lamina), whereas as the opposite or apical surface interacts with fluid or air (in the case of skin).
  • Neurons are derived from epithelial cell layers; they generally have a dendritic-axonal polarity.
  • The cells with an epithelial sheet are attached to one another through adherence junctions (adherens junction and desmosomes) and to the underlying extracellular matrix through hemidesmosomes and integrin-based adhesion sites.
 
  • Cell-cell and cell-matrix junctions are typically attachment points for cytoskeletal systems; this leads to polarized cytoskeletal organization.
  • Movement of fluid across an epithelial sheet is restricted by the presence of tight junctions between cells.
  • Movement of molecules between epithelial cells is mediated primarily by gap junctions, which link opposing membranes.
  • Mesenchymal cells are typically embedded within extracellular matrix.
  • Specialized mesenchymal cells, such as skeletal muscle cells, are surrounded by distinct extracellular matrix, the basal lamina.
  • Interactions between cells can lead to cytoskeletal reorganization and altered membrane traffic patterns.

Questions to ponder

  • What is the value or biological role of neuronal polarity?
  • What is the role the cytoskeleton in terms of polarized cell (e.g. neurons or epithelial cells) function
