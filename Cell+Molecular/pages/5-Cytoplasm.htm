Knowledge statements

  • Eukaryotic cells are characterized by a number of structurally and functionally discrete, membrane-defined compartments.
  • Such membrane-defined compartments occur less frequently in prokaryotic cells, and are typically derived from the plasma (boundary) membrane.
  • Polypeptide synthesis (translation) occurs within the cytoplasm and involves ribosomes (which contain a catalytically active RNA) and accessory factors (tRNAs and such).
  • Translation (which is not thermodynamically favorable) occurs through reaction-coupling to thermodynamically-favorable nucleotide triphosphate hydrolysis reactions.

  
  • Proteins, which are functional entities, are often composed of multiple polypeptides and non-polypeptide co-factors and ions. A gene typically encodes a single polypeptide (ignoring the complexities of alternative splicing, etc. for the moment).
  • Because the active (functional) state of a protein is often not much more stable (thermodynamically) than other kinetically accessible states, molecular chaperones can act to regulate protein activity by stabilizing the active state.
  • In many cases the formation of a protein involves a number of polypeptides together with non-polypeptide cofactors. The assembly process often involves molecular chaperones, which help regulate folding and assembly.
  • Molecular chaperones are themselves often complex proteins.
  • Molecular chaperones do not determine the final folded state of a polypeptide or protein, but rather recognize aberrantly folded polypeptides and proteins, unfold them (in a ATP-dependent process), and then release them, allowing them to refold.
  • Molecular chaperones often recognize misfolded proteins by the presence of hydrophobic residues on their surface.
  • One class of chaperone mediates the synthesis of polypeptides that compose membrane or secreted proteins.
  • Misfolded polypeptides and proteins are recognized and degraded by proteases.
  • Within the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells are dynamic polymer networks, which together form the cytoskeleton.
  • These cytoskeletal polymers are involved in moving membranes, organelles and chromosomes.
  • The polarity of the cell can influence cytoskeletal organization, and cytoskeletal organization, in turn, can influence cellular polarity (asymmetry).
  • Movements can involve controlled polymerization/depolymerization reactions or molecular motors that can move along polymers while attached to “cargo” (often membranes).
  • These motile reactions involved coupled NTP hydrolysis reactions and polymer asymmetries.
  • Symmetric polymers (e.g. intermediate filaments) are not involve in motile processes but appear to be involved in primarily mechanical processes.
  • That said, all cytoplasmic polymers have the potential to act as scaffolds, organizing molecules on their surfaces.

Questions to ponder

  • In the context of cell theory, which came first, the protein or the chaperone required to fold it?
  • Why is polypeptide assembly not a thermodynamically favorable reaction?
  • Why is polypeptide folding thermodynamically favorable?
  • Why are chaperones required during the process of membrane protein synthesis?
  • How would you determine if the assembly of a particular protein were thermodynamically favorable or unfavorable?
  • How might cytoskeletal polymerization and depolymerization move a membrane? What accessory proteins would you predict to be involved?
  • How does your model compare to a model by which cytoskeletal polymers find and segregate chromosomes during mitosis or cleave a cell into two?

  • Explain in terms of potential energies, why chaperone function must depend on coupled, thermodynamically favorable reactions.

  • Using diagrams, imagine motile events based on polymer assembly and disassembly and constrast them to those involving molecular motors moving along a polymer.
  • Predict (and illustrate) the types of organization you might expect to see in polymer systems with structural versus motile function and the thermodynamic factors involved in each.
  • A number of mitochondrial proteins are assembled in the cytoplasm and transported into the inner mitochondrial matrix; illustrate a plausible scenario (noting the thermodynamic obstacles involved) in having such a protein reach the matrix.
