Knowledge Statements

  • Within all populations of organisms, there is phenotypic variation (phenotype = all of an organism’s observable traits) between individuals.
    Some of this phenotypic variation is due to hereditable factors (genotype).
  • An organism can, in theory, produce many offspring.
  • Variation within a population can influence reproductive success, that is the number of viable and fertile offspring produced by an organism.
  • Mutations (changes in an organism’s genotype) occur spontaneously, due to the chemical nature of the genetic material and its interactions with the environment. These genotypic changes can lead to phenotypic changes.
  • Over time, differences in reproductive success between organisms can lead to changes in the genotypes (genes and the various versions present within an organism) and the phenotypes of organisms. This process is known as natural selection.
 
  • Sexual selection, a variant of natural selection, arises due to differential reproductive strategies of male and female sexes.
  • In many types of organisms, natural selection involves more than simply the reproductive success of individuals; it includes the effects of interactions on others that share genotypes. This is known as “inclusive fitness”.
  • Which phenotypes (and so genotypes) are selected reflects environmental factors (including climate change, the emergence of pathogens etc.), and so changes over time.
  • Survival (and therefore reproductive success) is often dramatically influenced by random events, which in turn can lead to bottlenecks and founder effects. Genetic drift is also in play (as a result of random death and genetic inheritance mechanisms)(expand?)
  • The effects of selection, drift, and other random events can lead to the reproductive isolation between once unified populations; over time this produces new species.
  • Evidence for evolutionary relationship can be found at all levels of biological organization, as well as within the fossil record.
  • All extant (and fossil) organisms appear to be derived from a single common ancestor.
  • An important practical implication of evolutionary mechanisms are organisms is historic entities, shaped by their ancestors and both selective and random processes.

Questions to ponder

  • If you found life on another planet, what features would you (reasonably) expect to find?
  • Is evolution a common feature of all types of organisms, or are there exceptions, and if so how do they occur?
  • How might different genotypes produce a common phenotype?
  • How might one determine whether a trait observed in two different types of organisms was present in their common ancestor?
  • Under what conditions would you expect inclusive fitness to play a significant role in evolutionary change?
  • Under what conditions would you expect to find sexual selection occurring?
  • What limits the effects of sexual selection?

Performance Expectations

  • Consider a particular organism, propose an evolutionary model that explains its unique phenotypic features, together with evidence you would use to support your hypotheses.
  • Describe the evidence you would look for to decide whether a trait was selected or was due to a genetic drift/bottleneck effect.
  • Generate a timeline for the divergence of different major types of organisms. What evidence is it based on, where might selective pressures be expected to play a prominent role.
  • Argue the case, random events (genetic drift and bottlenecks) are critical for the appearance of evolutionary novelty.
