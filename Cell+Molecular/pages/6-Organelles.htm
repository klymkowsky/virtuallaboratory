Knowledge statements

  • In prokaryotic cells, there is no physical (membrane) barrier between the genomic DNA and its associated proteins; the region occupied by the genomic DNA is known as the nucleoid.
  • Because of this lack of separation, nascent transcripts can begin to be translated before transcription is complete; this is not possible in eukaryotic cells.
  • In eukaryotic cells, genomic DNA (and its associated proteins) are separated from the cytoplasm by the nuclear envelope complex; transcription and translation are discrete.

  
  • RNA processing (regulated splicing, 5’ cap addition, and 3’ polyadenylation) are common within eukaryotic cells.
    Within the nucleus, genes encoding ribosomal RNAs are often aggregated into a specific region, the nucleolus; preliminary assembly of ribosomes occurs within this nuclear region.
  • Nuclear pores mediate the passive and active transport of molecules into and out of the nucleus; active transport processes involve NLS (nuclear localization) and NES (nuclear exclusion) signals as well as coupled NTP hydrolysis reactions.
  • A particular protein can contain both NLS and NES sequences; their relative activities can be modulated post-translationally.
  • The endoplasmic reticulum emerges from, and is continuous with the nuclear envelope; it is the site of the translation of many integral, lumen, and secreted proteins.
  • The Golgi apparatus is involve in the post-translational processing and sorting of membrane, lumenal, and secreted proteins.
  • Vesicle formation and fusion events are mediated by associated proteins and receptors; transfer between endoplasmic reticulum and the Golgi Apparatus involves vesicles.

  • Vesicles, derived from the plasma membrane (endosomes), interact with intracellular membrane compartments (lysosomes, autophagosomes, peroxisomes, and endoplasmic reticlulum to deliver membrane associated proteins and to recycle membrane proteins.
  • Mitochondria and chloroplasts are discrete from other cytoplasmic organelles in that they have double membranes and their own (albeit reduced) circular genomes. This reflects their endosymbiotic origins.
