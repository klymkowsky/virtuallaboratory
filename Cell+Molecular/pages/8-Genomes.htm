Knowledge statements

  • The genome of an organism is all of the DNA it contains.
  • Prokaryotes have a major, often circular DNA molecule and smaller molecules (often known as plasmids).
  • Eukaryotes typically have multiple, linear DNA molecules (chromosomes) located within the nucleus, and circular mitochondrial, and in the case of plants, chloroplast/plastids.

  
  • While cells use double stranded DNA to encode genetic information, viruses (which are non-metabolic, obligate parasites can use dsDNA, single stranded DNA, dsRNA or single stranded RNA (depending on the virus.
  • Typically distinct DNA molecules are known as chromosomes (an anachronism).
  • A gene is a region along the DNA molecule; it contains the sequence elements involved in regulating gene expression as well as the sequence that is expressed, that is transcribed into RNA (see Gene expression).
  • Genes can overlap and their expression can interfere with one another
  • Because of their lengths (many time the diameter of the cells in which they exist), chromosomes must be packaged. This involves interactions with accessory proteins, histones in eukaryotes.
  • The way a DNA molecule is packaged can influence whether the genes it contains are expressible.
  • Genomes are not static. They can undergo duplication, either in toto or in part. They can also undergo more complex reorganization. Mutations can lead to the loss of functional genes or the appearance of new genes.
  • Much of the genome, particularly of eukaryotes, consists of “self” elements (related to retroviruses). These elements are inactivated during evolution, but can be important drives of genomic evolution in their own right, particularly in concert with molecular mechanisms.
  • Genetic variation, through its effects of reproductive success, it the target of selection pressures.
  • Mutations which can occur for simple chemical reactions (associated with the properties of DNA), mistakes during replication, or in response to environmental insult are generally repaired, although not always correctly.
  • Mutations that disrupt the organism’s error repair system lead to an increased frequency of mutation.
  • Chromosomes are not static, they can fuse with one another, split into two (or more).
  • In sexual organisms, in which chromosomes align during meiosis, changes in genome organization can lead to reproductive isolation from other organisms. Reproductive isolation can lead to extinction or speciation.
